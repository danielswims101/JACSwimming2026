Energy Systems

AEROBIC THRESHOLD

The foundation of elite swimming. Training below 3 mmol/L lactate builds the aerobic engine that fuels championship performances. Your heart rate cues are 26–28 on the Golf HR scale for steady-state AeT work.

  • Sub-threshold work: 1.5–2.5 mmol
  • AeT zone: 2.5–3 mmol
  • Threshold: 3–4 mmol — use sparingly in early meso
  • DB (fingerstick) testing validates actual lactate values
Periodization

MESO STRUCTURE

Each meso-cycle builds on the last. Weeks 1–3 establish aerobic base by stroke. Week 4 introduces threshold. Meso 2 stacks volume and begins crossover between energy systems.

  • Wk 1–3: Stroke-specific AeT focus
  • Wk 4: Threshold introduction (6×400)
  • Meso 2: Volume + intensity crossover
  • Recovery days preserve adaptation
Equipment System

TOOL PROGRESSION

Equipment isn't optional — it's programming. Each tool has a specific physiological purpose and is deliberately sequenced within sessions to build feel, power, and efficiency.

  • Snorkel: isolates stroke without breath timing
  • Fins: overspeed training + dolphin kick loading
  • Paddle on top: proprioceptive catch feedback
  • Band pull: hip drive and high elbow pull

ENTRY & CATCH — The Power Position

▾

Elite freestyle starts before the pull. The hand enters narrow — inside the shoulder line, fingertips first at a slight downward angle, no splash. The arm extends fully forward in the water before engaging the catch, allowing the shoulder to rotate fully over the centerline. Caeleb Dressel enters at 11 o'clock and 1 o'clock; never wider. The "reach" phase is not passive — you're extending from the hip girdle and lats, loading the catch before the hand moves backward at all.

🎯 Coaching Cue: "Reach around a barrel" — your high elbow sets before the hand ever pulls back. Press the elbow out, not down.
Entry angle

Hand enters 10–15° below horizontal, never flat or over-rotated

Elbow position

Elbow stays above wrist throughout entire catch phase

Catch depth

Wrist bends to vertical at ~30–40cm depth before pull initiates

Elite benchmark

Catch angle of wrist to forearm: 90° or less at max catch depth

ROTATION & BODY POSITION

▾

Body rotation in elite freestyle is driven from the hips, not the shoulders. The rotation angle for sprint events is 35–45°; for distance it's 25–35°. Rotation is timed with the kick — when the left hand catches, the right hip drives down. This is not a conscious "roll" — it's a result of a powerful 2-beat or 6-beat kick that torques the hips and loads the pulling arm's lat muscle before the pull begins.

🎯 Coaching Cue: "Your hips drive your arms, not the other way around." If you're tired and losing rotation, your kick has died first.
Head position

Hairline at surface, eyes looking at pool bottom — NOT forward

Hips at surface

Hips should be visible near surface — sinking hips add massive drag

Shoulder log roll

Shoulders rotate as a unit — avoid "independent shoulder" wobble

Foot drag test

Feet should leave a shallow wake, not splash out to the sides

THE PULL PATTERN — S-Pull vs Straight Pull

▾

Modern elite freestyle uses a modified straight-path pull — the hand tracks relatively straight beneath the body centerline with subtle outward-inward adjustments. The old exaggerated S-pull is obsolete. What matters is maximum "still water" contact: the hand should feel resistance throughout the entire pull from catch to hip. When you push through dead water (water you already moved), you lose propulsion. Olympic swimmers like Sarah Sjöström accelerate the hand from catch to finish — slowest at the catch, fastest at the hip.

🎯 Coaching Cue: "Paint the lane line black from your hip to your thigh." The finish is where most age-group swimmers quit too early.

The finish extends past the hip — wrist turns outward and thumb points down as the hand exits. Exiting too early (at the belly button) cuts power by ~30%.

Pull path

Near-straight line under body centerline — avoid wide sweeping

Acceleration

Hand speed increases from catch to finish — never decelerates mid-pull

Exit point

Hand exits at the thigh — fingers facing back, wrist supinated

Elbow recovery

High elbow recovery over water for 200m+; allowed lower for sprint

GOLF SCORE — Your AeT Pacing Tool

▾

Golf Score = Split (seconds for 50m) + Stroke Count per 50. Used in your program for AeT tech sets. A 100m swimmer with a goal of 30-stroke/30-second 50m targets a golf score of 60. The goal is to find the combination of stroke rate and DPS (distance per stroke) that minimizes the score. Elite swimmers optimize golf score, then gradually increase tempo while holding or improving it.

🎯 Your Program: Tech sets use "Des 1-5 to Golf BA" — Descend 1–5 sets to your Golf Best Average, meaning each rep you tune stroke rate slightly until finding the fastest-split/lowest-count combination.
Target range

Elite age-group: Golf score 55–70 depending on event

Recording

Log split + count every rep — weekly trends reveal improvements

DPS +2 drill

Try to swim 2 fewer strokes per length than your normal count

HR target

AeT tech work: HR 26–28 on golf HR scale (zone 2 effort)

BODY POSITION & THE DUCK

▾

The "Duck" referenced in your program is a backstroke-specific technique tool — a float/collar device that keeps your head in correct alignment. Correct backstroke position has hips at the surface, ears submerged, eyes looking straight up or slightly toward your feet. Ryan Murphy's hips are so high his knees barely break the surface on the kick. Any hip drop immediately increases drag exponentially.

🎯 Coaching Cue: "Squeeze a coin between your glutes and keep your belly button pointed at the ceiling." Hip drop is almost always caused by a weak core or lack of body awareness.
Head neutral

No chin tuck, no looking at feet — one ear depth below surface

Kick plane

Kick must originate from hip, not knee — feet barely break surface

Shoulder rotation

45–55° roll each stroke — more rotation = more lat engagement

Duck drill

The duck provides proprioceptive feedback on neck and head alignment in real time

CATCH & PULL — The Pinky Entry

▾

Backstroke hand entry is pinky-first, hand rotated so the thumb is pointing in toward the centerline. The arm enters directly above the shoulder — never crossing the centerline (creates a snake-body effect) and never wider than the shoulder line (wastes rotation energy). From entry, the arm extends fully before the catch begins — reach past your head, feel the lats engage, then initiate the catch with a high elbow pushing out to the side.

🎯 Coaching Cue (2-2-2 Drill): Two strokes right arm only, two strokes left arm only, two strokes full. Used extensively in your program for isolating and feeling the catch without tempo pressure.
Entry

Pinky leads, arm enters at 12–1 o'clock position (shoulder-width)

Catch depth

Hand descends to ~45cm before pressing outward to begin pull

Pull path

S-curve is more prominent in backstroke — press out, sweep in, push back

Exit

Thumb exits first at the hip, elbow recovers bent over the body

BACKSTROKE KICK & TEMPO

▾

Backstroke relies on a 6-beat kick — 6 kicks per stroke cycle. The kick is narrow (feet no wider than hip-width), fast, and driven from the hip flexors. The upward kick phase is propulsive — toes pointed, foot faces upward, ankle plantarflexed. The downbeat is recovery. Kick training on your back (as your program prescribes) with fins loads the hip flexors and builds the tempo endurance needed for race pace.

🎯 "Heavy Fish Kick" drill in your warm-ups: exaggerated undulation on your side with a full dolphin kick pattern — bridges backstroke kick mechanics to the rotation rhythm needed for fast backstroke.
Kick tempo

Elite BK kick: 150–180 kicks/min (6-beat cycle matches stroke rate)

Ankle flex

Flexible ankles are non-negotiable — daily ankle stretching required

Depth

Kick depth: 30–40cm from surface to toe tip — too deep wastes energy

Head stability

Head stays perfectly still even as body rotates — use ceiling tile as reference

THE 2K1 SCULL — Your Program's Core Drill

▾

"2k1s" (2 kicks, 1 stroke) is the foundational breaststroke drill in your program. It forces the swimmer to feel the glide and catch every cycle by doubling the kick before each pull, slowing tempo and isolating the pull initiation. Elite breaststrokers like Qin Haiyang use an extremely patient out-scull before the catch — the hands move OUT before they go down. Most age-group swimmers rush this and collapse the elbows inward too early.

🎯 Coaching Cue: "Deep 2k1 scull / Shallow 2k1 scull / Flat rhythm" — your program progressively decreases the scull depth across the set, transitioning from drill feel to race feel.
Scull path

Hands press out, then in, then sweep back — full heart-shaped pattern

Deep version

Hands go below chest level — exaggerates feel for water pressure against palm

Flat version

Hands stay at surface plane — matches actual race technique timing

Transition

"Flat connect" drills link drill feel directly into full-speed swimming

BREASTSTROKE KICK — The Engine

▾

Breaststroke kick accounts for 60–70% of propulsion. The heels draw up narrow (not wide — this creates drag), feet flex outward (eversion — think duck feet), then sweep out-and-around in a circular pattern, finishing with feet together. The most common flaw is a "bicycle kick" where one foot drops and sweeps differently than the other. Your kick sets (10×100 BR kick, descend) build the specific endurance and feel needed for race-speed kick.

🎯 "BR pull make" sets in your program: these isolate the pull-only pattern using a pull buoy — if you can't feel propulsion from your arms alone in breaststroke, your elbow position is collapsing during the in-sweep.
Recovery width

Heels recover no wider than hip-width — wider = drag spike

Foot position

Feet flex outward (dorsiflexed + everted) before the sweep

Sweep path

Circular — out, around, and together in one continuous motion

Glide

Full extension after kick — both hands and feet together, streamlined

TIMING — The Most Overlooked Element

▾

The pull and kick in breaststroke must never overlap. The sequence is: PULL → breathe → kick → glide. Any overlap creates opposing forces. The breath happens during the in-sweep when the hands come inward and the elbows tuck — the head doesn't pop up, it rides the natural body lift created by the pull. Adam Peaty's head barely clears the surface even in sprint races. The DPS / DPS+2 / DPS+4 sets in your program systematically challenge timing by reducing stroke count.

🎯 Build each 25 rule (+1, +2, +3, +4 effort level per 25): Each 25 of a 100 builds in effort and tempo — this trains breaststrokers to maintain timing under race-velocity fatigue.

UNDULATION — The Wave From the Ground Up

▾

Butterfly is not an arm-driven stroke. The movement initiates from the chest pressing down, which causes the hips to rise, which causes the feet to kick down. It's a wave. Michael Phelps' butterfly is often described as "dolphin-like" because his undulation starts at the chest and travels through his hips with no interruption. The 2-2-2 drill used extensively in your program teaches swimmers to feel each phase in isolation before connecting them.

🎯 2-2-2 Drill (from your program): 2 right arm, 2 left arm, 2 full butterfly. One dolphin kick per arm stroke in drill mode. This isolates the one-arm pull pattern and makes asymmetries obvious.
Wave initiation

Chest DOWN first — hips rise as a consequence, not simultaneously

Kick timing

First kick: at entry/extension. Second kick: at the push/exit phase

Amplitude

Hip undulation ~20–30cm — too much = drag, too little = no propulsion

OA drill

One-arm fly isolates catch while the free arm provides balance — "3-3 alternating" in your program

BUTTERFLY CATCH & NO-SPLASH ENTRY

▾

"No splash" fly is a key drill category in your program (Wiff — a resistance-free paddle that forces clean water entry). If a butterfly swimmer makes a big splash on hand entry, they are creating drag and losing the "feel" for the catch. Elite butterfly swimmers like Sarah Sjöström enter with almost zero surface disturbance — the hands slice in at a 45° downward angle, thumbs slightly down, arms wider than the shoulders but not dramatically so.

🎯 "Wiff" tool: The resistance-free paddle makes splash immediately obvious. If you hear noise on entry, your hands are entering flat — adjust angle and aim for silence.
Entry width

Hands enter at roughly shoulder-to-1.5x shoulder width

Entry angle

45° downward, slight pinky-up rotation — never flat or palms-down

Breath timing

Head forward (not up) — chin barely clears the surface to breathe

Padd on top

Paddle balanced on back of hand — teaches wrist position and flat hand at catch

BUTTERFLY KICK — Amplitude & Frequency

▾

For race butterfly, the second kick (at the hip exit) is the propulsive kick — this is what drives the swimmer forward. The first kick (at entry) is primarily for maintaining body position and initiating undulation. Kick depth shouldn't be excessive: elite swimmers kick 35–45cm amplitude. Training butterfly kick on your back (as your program uses with fins in warm-up) allows the swimmer to feel the kick motion without managing body position simultaneously.

🎯 Kick on back drill (with fins): Lying on your back kicking butterfly kick allows you to see your knee bend and foot plantar flexion. Knees should barely break the surface on the upstroke.

UNDERWATER DOLPHIN KICK — The 5th Stroke

▾

Underwater dolphin kick is widely considered the fastest way for humans to travel through water. At elite levels, swimmers often hold higher velocity underwater than on the surface. Your program features UW 15m on nearly every session — 15m is the legal maximum. Caeleb Dressel is considered one of the best underwater kickers in history; his kick velocity off the wall exceeds his surface sprint pace.

The kick is driven from the hips — the body forms a wave from the chest through the hips to the feet. The amplitude is tight: 25–35cm. The kick rate in elite swimmers reaches 180–210 kicks per minute. The key to maintaining speed is a streamline that creates zero drag — arms locked overhead, hands stacked, core rigid.

🎯 Your Program: "UW 15 IM Drill @ 1:00" sets — each rep you maximize depth hold, streamline tightness, and kick tempo before breaking out. This is where races are won and lost.
Streamline

Hands stacked (dominant on top), arms squeezed past ears, core locked — not a "V" shape

Kick depth

30–40cm amplitude — hips generate, not knees. Knees bend ~30–40° only

Depth control

Elite: 0.5–0.6m below surface — deeper creates more drag, shallower loses power

Breakout timing

Breakout occurs before velocity drops below surface swim speed — usually at 12–14m

TURNS — Where Races Are Won

▾

"Fast flip" is mentioned explicitly in your freestyle warm-ups ("Fr Neg split last 50 build to fast flip"). A fast flip turn involves an aggressive approach (don't slow down into the wall), a compact somersault rotation, two-footed plant on the wall at 0.4–0.6m depth, and an explosive push into a tight streamline. The push off the wall is the fastest moment in any race — elite swimmers reach 7.5+ m/s at push-off. Every tenth of a second lost in the turn is a tenth you must make up swimming.

🎯 Neg Split + Fast Flip drill: Swim the first 50 of a 100 at AeT effort, then the second 50 building to a maximum-effort flip turn. This trains the neuromuscular pattern of attacking turns under fatigue.
Approach

Maintain or accelerate into the wall — never decelerate in final 5m

Flip distance

Initiate flip 1.2–1.5 body lengths from wall — varies by swimmer

Push depth

Push at 0.4–0.6m depth — not too shallow (drag) or too deep (too far to surface)

BR pullout

Breaststroke pullout: one underwater pull + one dolphin kick before breakout

Elite dryland training for swimmers is built around four principles: scapular stability, rotational power, hip hinge strength, and core anti-rotation. These mirror the exact demands of all four strokes. Every exercise below has a direct in-water counterpart.

STRENGTH RATIOS

  • Pull-up max: bodyweight + 20% for males, BW for females at minimum
  • Lat pulldown: 1.2× bodyweight for males, 0.85× for females

Injury Prevention

COMMON ISSUES

  • Swimmer's shoulder: usually serratus weakness — address with serratus punches daily
  • Breaststroker's knee: hip adductor imbalance — Copenhagen planks 3x/week
  • Low back pain: anterior pelvic tilt from hip flexor tightness — daily 90/90 hip work
  • Bicep tendon strain: overload from band pull sets — progress pull volume slowly
  • Ankle impingement: insufficient plantarflexion ROM — daily loading is essential
